A New Gas Chromatograph

This year, I was invited to work with Dr. Robert Senter in the LMU Chemistry Department on a new type of gas chromatograph detector (shown in top image). Rather than the expensive thin-filament RTDs used in many teaching lab instruments, our system is based on the V/T relationship of a diode when current is held constant.

In addition to designing the sensor driver and small signal amplifier, I also modified an old gas chromatograph (shown in bottom image) for use with our new sensor.

The diode detectors we have built have many advantages that make them uniquely suited for use in a teaching lab. They are rugged, do not require helium as a carrier gas, and inexpensive to make and use.

Robotic Bartender

Development of this project can be broken into two major versions. Version 1 was built around an an Arduino Uno and was able to mix up to 6 ingredients at a time. It also supported individual passcodes for access control.

Version 2, shown in the attached pictures at two different stages of development, is based on the Intel Curie SOC and can mix up to 10 ingredients. It also offers users the option to carbonate the entire beverage in a CNC-milled pre-dispensing chamber.

Recipies for Version 2 are defined using an accompanying Mac desktop app (which I wrote in Swift). Each user is able to create a local account using any card with a magnetic stripe as their unique user ID. After swiping a card associated with an account, recipes can be created and saved by moving sliders around. Volume dispensed under each account is recorded so that guests can be charged appropriately at the end of the event.

This project taught me most of what I know about CNC machining and C/C++ programming. All parts, software, and circuit designs are completely original.

OneWire for Arduino

This was actually a motor interface controller I designed for Version 1 of the robotic bartender to make better use of the limited I/O ports on the Arduino Uno. It allows one microcontroller output pin to control up to 31 output devices by automatically detecting incomming serial data and writing it to a bank of four 74HC595 shift registers.

After the first prototype was successful, I decided to design and order a PCB (shown in bottom picture). The project was discontinued when I found integrated circuits that implement a similar communication protocol, but I learned a lot from designing my own solution.

Classic Instruments

As a college student, I can't afford to buy lab-grade instruments for personal use. To get around this, I frequently buy broken or defective instruments (oscilliscopes, DMMs, LC meters, etc.) on eBay with the intention of fixing and calibrating them for personal use (or resale). I have accumulated nearly a complete set of old Tektronix instruments that I have used extensively when testing out new designs.

The instrument shown on the right is a 1969 Tektronix 453 oscilloscope that I fixed a couple months ago. Despite being 50 years old, it is an amazing piece of technology and has become my favorite tool for working on old electonic equipment, especially high voltage vacuum tube circuits.

Breadboard Nixie Tubes

Freshman year of college, I became fascinated with old style numeric displays (nixie tubes), and bought several to experiment with. To get the 160 VDC needed to drive them, I built a simple buck-boost converter from a topology published online. I used the only inductors and capacitors I had on hand and programmed an Arduino to control the switching until I found a frequency that worked. I then replaced the Arduino with a 555 timer configured to switch at the same frequency (shown in picture).
